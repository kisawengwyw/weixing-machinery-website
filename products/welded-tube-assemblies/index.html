Welded tube assemblies are drawing-specific rigid fluid-routing components made from straight or bent tube, machined weld fittings, bosses, flanges and mounting brackets. They are used to hold a fixed tube route and are not ordinary structural weldments. Pressure-boundary tube joints, structural bracket welds and connection-end sealing interfaces must be identified separately in the drawing and manufacturing requirements. Integrated welded structures can reduce some detachable joints, while JIC, ORFS, BSP, NPT, flange or drawing-specified ends still provide the sealing interface.

Technical Guide

Welded Tube Assemblies Technical Guide

Welded tube assemblies are not ordinary structural weldments. Pressure-boundary welds connect pressurized tubes, fittings, bosses or flanges, while structural or bracket welds attach mounting supports or non-pressure features. A weld that attaches a mounting bracket is not automatically qualified as a pressure-boundary weld.

The welding process is selected according to the base material, wall thickness, joint design, filler requirement, shielding or purge requirement and drawing-approved procedure. Welding procedure or qualification requirements are followed only when specified by the drawing, order or approved manufacturing plan.

Visual weld inspection and dimensional inspection do not by themselves verify pressure integrity. Leak, proof-pressure, burst, impulse or nondestructive testing is arranged only when specified by the drawing, order or validation plan, and the required inspection method and acceptance criteria must be specified by the drawing or order.

Post-weld work should remove chips, spatter, loose oxides and residual contamination. Internal cleanliness requirements, flushing, particle inspection and end protection are performed only as specified by the drawing, order or system requirement.

Weld heat input may change angle, end distance, centerline, roundness and flatness. Fixtures, welding sequence and intermediate inspection help control distortion; incorrect positions should not be corrected by forced bending or forced assembly. Final dimensions are checked to drawing datums, port orientation, mounting points and key interfaces.

Surface treatment must be confirmed by material and assembly structure. Sealing faces, threads, internal tube surfaces and fitted areas may require masking or protection, and post-weld coatings require prior removal of slag, spatter and oxides.

Materials and Surface Finish Options

Materials and Surface Finish Options

Material grades and combinations are confirmed according to the drawing, media, temperature, corrosion exposure, weldability and order requirements. Tube material, fitting material and weld-end design should be compatible with the selected joining process.

Suitable carbon-steel parts may use trivalent zinc plating or zinc-nickel coating, stainless steel may use passivation or natural finish, and other coatings are used only when specified by the drawing or order.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a welded tube assembly?
It is a drawing-specific rigid fluid-routing component made from straight or bent tube, machined weld fittings, bosses, flanges and mounting brackets. Pressure-boundary tube joints, structural bracket welds and connection-end sealing interfaces must be identified separately.

How is it different from a hydraulic hose assembly?
A welded tube assembly is a fixed rigid route for equipment installation, while a hose assembly is flexible and normally used where movement, vibration or service replacement is required.

Are all welds pressure-boundary welds?
No. A weld that attaches a mounting bracket is not automatically qualified as a pressure-boundary weld. Drawings should identify which joints retain hydraulic pressure and which joints provide only mechanical support.

Which welding process is used?
The process is selected according to material, wall thickness, joint design, filler, shielding or purge requirements and drawing-approved manufacturing requirements. One welding process is not automatically suitable for every assembly.

What materials are available?
Carbon steel and stainless steel are typical options. Other weldable materials are reviewed according to drawing, joint design, media, pressure, corrosion exposure and welding-process requirements.

How is working pressure confirmed?
Pressure is confirmed according to tube material and grade, outside diameter, wall thickness, joint design, welding requirements, connection ends, media, temperature, system duty and the specified inspection or test plan.

Are all assemblies pressure tested?
No blanket test claim is made. Leak, proof-pressure, burst, impulse or other testing is arranged only when specified by the drawing, order or validation plan.

How are welding distortion and port orientation controlled?
Fixtures, welding sequence and in-process checks help control angle, end distance, centerline, roundness and flatness. Incorrect position should not be corrected by forced bending or forced assembly.

How is internal cleanliness handled?
Chips, spatter, loose oxides and residual contamination are removed after welding. Internal cleanliness, flushing, particle inspection and end protection are performed only as specified by the drawing, order or system requirement.

What surface treatments are available?
Post-weld cleaning and material-compatible finishes may include trivalent zinc plating or zinc-nickel coating for suitable carbon-steel parts, passivation or natural finish for stainless steel, or another drawing-specified coating.

What drawing information is required?
Provide the latest drawing revision, assembly datums, tube route, bend radii, end types, sealing structures, weld locations, brackets, media, pressure, finish, cleanliness and inspection requirements.

Can assemblies be manufactured from samples?
Samples can support quotation and reverse-checking, but drawings, models and assembly information are recommended for stable repeat production.
